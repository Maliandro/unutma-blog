A literature review can start with one paper and become a maze by lunchtime.

You open a promising article. It cites three classics. Those classics cite a debate. A newer paper challenges the debate. Someone on X recommends eleven tools. Suddenly you have 47 tabs and no idea which one matters.

The solution is not reading faster. It is separating discovery from decision-making.

## Step 1: Start with one seed paper

Pick one strong paper that is close to your research question. It does not have to be perfect. It only needs to be good enough to act as a seed.

Put that paper into a mapping tool such as Connected Papers, Litmaps, or ResearchRabbit. Your goal is to identify clusters:

- Foundational papers
- Recent papers
- Contradicting papers
- Method papers
- Review papers

Do not try to read everything yet.

## Step 2: Build a paper queue

Create a paper queue with three levels:

- Must read
- Maybe read
- Reference only

Most papers should not enter "must read." If everything is urgent, the queue becomes useless.

For each must-read paper, capture the title, link, why it matters, and which chapter or question it belongs to.

## Step 3: Read for a purpose

Before reading, write one question:

"What do I need from this paper?"

Examples:

- Does this support my main claim?
- Is the method useful?
- Is this a competing theory?
- Does this belong in the background section?

That question protects you from passive highlighting.

## Step 4: Use AI summaries carefully

Tools like SciSpace, Elicit, and Consensus can help you scan a field, but summaries are not citations. Use them to decide what to read, then verify the actual paper.

If a claim matters in your thesis, it deserves source-level checking.

## Step 5: Review weekly

Once a week, review your queue:

- Move irrelevant papers out
- Promote important papers
- Turn notes into thesis tasks
- Identify missing evidence
